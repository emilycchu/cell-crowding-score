Here's what each panel in the 6-image diagnostic grid (from save_visualization in scripts/explore_crenation.py) shows, for a given FOV:

1. Raw FOV (top-left) — the original grayscale image, unmodified, for visual reference.
2. Raw Otsu mask (top-middle) — the binary foreground/background mask straight out of otsu_segment, before any illumination correction or cleanup. This is the noisy, speckled version driven partly by the illumination gradient.
3. Cleaned mask (open + area filter, illumination-corrected) (top-right) — the mask after flattening the illumination gradient (correct_illumination) and applying light morphological opening + a minimum-area filter (clean_mask), with closing deliberately skipped so the small inter-cell gaps survive.
4. Gap contour (red) vs. convex hull (green) (bottom-left) — every contour find_blob_contours kept (the background gaps enclosed within the merged foreground blob, i.e. spaces between touching cells) drawn in red, overlaid with each one's convex hull in green. Where red and green diverge is where a shape is non-convex — the raw material both descriptors are computed from.
5. Largest gap: radius vs. angle bin (bottom-middle) — for the single largest gap contour, the radial profile: distance from that contour's centroid, sampled at 360 evenly spaced angles around it. This is the raw 1D signal that gets Fourier-transformed.
6. Largest gap: radial FFT power (crenation band shaded) (bottom-right) — the power spectrum (FFT) of that radial profile, with the expected crenation frequency band (8–20 cycles/perimeter) shaded in red. A real crenation ripple would show a distinct peak inside that shaded band; what you'd expect from ordinary noise/packing irregularity is power concentrated near the low-frequency/left side of the plot instead.
